What is sequence alignment, in plain English?

Living things store their instructions as long strings of letters. DNA uses 4 letters (A, C, G, T), the chemical "bases" that spell out the double helix. Proteins, the molecular machines that do almost all the work in a cell, are written in 20 letters, one for each amino acid building block. A gene is a stretch of DNA; the cell reads it three letters at a time (each triplet is a codon) and builds the matching protein.

Aligning two sequences means sliding them past each other to line up the parts that match, adding dashes (gaps) where one has letters the other is missing. Biologists do this because similar sequences usually share a common ancestor. Life copies its DNA to reproduce, copying makes occasional typos (mutations), and over millions of years those changes add up. Line two sequences up and the matches show what stayed the same (often because it is important and evolution could not afford to break it), while the differences show where the two drifted apart. Alignment is how we measure how related two things are, find genes, and spot which mutations actually matter.

Here is the catch this whole project is about. To line sequences up, a computer first needs numbers: how much is a match worth? how much does a mismatch cost? how much does a gap cost? Change those numbers and you get a different alignment out. This tool hands you those knobs so you can see it happen, and then asks the hard question: if the answer depends on the knobs, how do you know which alignment is right?

Two quick terms you will see below: global alignment lines the sequences up end to end (best when they are the same kind of thing overall); local alignment finds just the single best-matching stretch and ignores the rest (best when they only share a piece). Both are explained with a live example further down.

The lesson

A higher score is not a better alignment

The tool always returns the highest-scoring alignment for the numbers you set. But "highest-scoring" is not the same as "biologically right." Watch what happens when we make gaps almost free: the computer discovers it can dodge nearly every mismatch by inserting a tiny gap next to it, chopping the alignment into a staircase of dashes. That earns a higher score, yet it is nonsense, no real gene picks up dozens of separate one-letter insertions and deletions.

The "bad" alignment wins on score but loses on every honest readout: its identity is lower and it is stuffed with gaps. That is the takeaway, the raw score is the computer's opinion given your numbers, not a measure of biological truth. (HBB, by the way, is hemoglobin beta, part of the protein that carries oxygen in your blood; it is nearly identical across mammals because a broken oxygen-carrier is not survivable, so evolution keeps it almost frozen.)

Global vs local

When local alignment wins: a shared piece inside two different sequences

Global alignment lines two sequences up from start to finish. Local alignment instead hunts for the single best-matching stretch and throws the rest away. That distinction is huge in biology, because two proteins that are mostly unrelated often still share one important part, called a domain: a self-contained working unit, a bit like the same style of handle bolted onto two completely different tools.

Below are two made-up proteins that share a 41-letter domain buried in the middle, with different lengths and different sequence on either side. Global alignment is forced to match the unrelated ends too, so it drags the shared part out of register and produces a gap-riddled mess with a negative score. Local alignment ignores the ends and lifts the shared domain out cleanly at 100% identity. Same two sequences, same scoring: the only thing that changed is global versus local.

Rule of thumb: use global when the two sequences should correspond along their whole length (the same gene in two species); use local when you only expect a shared piece (a common domain, a short motif, one region of similarity). This is exactly why BLAST, the most-used search tool in all of biology, is local: it can find the matching piece of a gene even when everything around it is different.

DNA vs protein

SARS-CoV vs SARS-CoV-2 spike: the synonymous-mutation story

The spike is the protein that studs the surface of a coronavirus and lets it break into your cells; it is the part your vaccine trains your immune system to recognize. Here we compare the spike of the original 2003 SARS virus with that of SARS-CoV-2, the COVID-19 virus, two related coronaviruses about two decades apart.

We align the spike two ways: first as coding DNA (the gene, in A/C/G/T), then as the protein it builds (in amino-acid letters). The point is a beautiful quirk of the genetic code: a change in the DNA does not always change the protein. Because most amino acids can be spelled by several codons, a mutation in the third letter of a codon often swaps one spelling for another that means the same amino acid. That is a synonymous ("silent") mutation, visible in the DNA, invisible in the protein.

Aligned spike codons
Nucleotide substitutions by codon position

The DNA alignment sees hundreds more differences than the protein does. Most of the codons that differ are synonymous (the DNA changed but the amino acid did not), and those changes pile up at the third letter of the codon (nicknamed the "wobble" position), exactly where the genetic code has the most spare spellings. This is the fingerprint of purifying selection: mutations that would change the protein tend to break it, so the individuals carrying them do worse and those mutations get filtered out of the population over time. Silent mutations pass through freely. The result is that the protein changes more slowly than the gene that encodes it, it stays more conserved.

The punchline: the DNA alignment counts how much the sequence changed; the protein alignment shows how much of that change could actually matter. Synonymous mutations are the gap between those two numbers. To find the mutations that matter, align the protein.

The central question

So how do you quantify alignment quality?

If the score itself moves with the parameters, what do you trust instead? The score is only the optimizer's objective, not a measure of truth. "Quality" asks a different question: did the alignment recover the real evolutionary or structural correspondence between the two sequences? Here are five checks, each grounded in biology, and most of them run right here.

1 · The raw score is not comparable across settings

Take one fixed alignment and rescore it under different parameters. Not a single letter moves, yet the number changes; doubling the match reward roughly doubles the score. So a bigger score never means a better alignment unless the scoring scheme is held fixed.

Example. The same human vs mouse HBB alignment (about 83% identical, essentially no gaps) scores 293 with match 1 but 1,773 with match 5. Identical biology, six times the number.

2 · Percent identity is useful, but only if you count the gaps

Percent identity is the fraction of the lined-up columns that match. It does not depend on the reward units, so it survives changing the scoring, which is good. But there is a subtle trap in how you count it. If you divide only by the columns where both sequences have a letter (ignoring the gap columns), then insertions and deletions become invisible, and you can push identity all the way to 100% just by inserting gaps around every mismatch. The honest definition, and the one this tool uses, divides by every column, gaps included, so each indel counts against the score. That is why the "bad" alignments above report a lower identity, not a fake perfect one.

Example. The gap-shredded human/mouse HBB alignment is "100% identical" if you ignore its 103 gaps, but only about 75% identical once you count them. Same alignment, two very different stories. Lesson: never read percent identity without the gap count sitting right next to it.

3 · Is it better than chance? (statistical significance)

A real alignment should score far above what random sequences of the same length and composition can reach. Shuffle one sequence many times, align each shuffle, and measure how many standard deviations the real score sits above that null distribution. This is the reasoning behind BLAST's E-value: the question is not "is the number big?" but "could this have happened by chance?"

4 · Does it respect the reading frame? (coding DNA)

Real insertions and deletions in a protein-coding gene almost always come in multiples of three, because a length change that is not a multiple of three shifts the reading frame and scrambles every downstream codon. An alignment of coding DNA littered with 1- and 2-base gaps is almost certainly a scoring artifact, however high it scores.

Example. In the SARS spike the two genes differ in length by 18 amino acids, which is exactly 54 nucleotides: an in-frame indel, precisely what selection on a functional protein predicts.

5 · Does it agree with biology you did not score for?

The strongest test is external agreement. Conserved motifs, catalytic residues, and cysteines that pair into disulfide bonds should line up; the alignment should match a structure-based superposition or a curated reference. None of that information is in the scoring, so agreement is genuine evidence.

Example. In the spike alignment the C-terminal ...KFDEDDSEPVLKGVKLHYT lines up perfectly in both viruses. The aligner was never told this region is conserved; it recovered a real biological anchor on its own.

Bottom line. There is no parameter-free definition of alignment quality, because alignment is inference (a hypothesis about shared ancestry), not measurement. The parameters are your evolutionary model: how often substitutions happen versus indels. A good alignment is one whose model fits the sequences and whose result survives the checks above; the score only tells you it was optimal for the model you chose. Quality is whether you chose the right model.

Stretch

Why gaps need smarter scoring

Try to find one gap penalty that both allows a single long, realistic indel and discourages lots of scattered short gaps. You cannot, and here is the proof.

My gap scoring is linear: a gap of length L costs g × L. So one gap of length 6 costs exactly the same as three scattered gaps of length 2. The scoring only sees total gap length, so it is blind to whether gaps are one event or scattered. Watch the tie:

Biology disagrees: a single 6-base indel is one mutational event, far likelier than six independent single-base indels. The fix is affine gaps: a big one-time open penalty plus a small extend penalty per extra base, so one long gap pays open once while many short gaps each pay it. Flip the tool’s gap model to Affine and re-run the “bad” presets to feel the difference.

Glossary

Every term on this page, in one line

Sequence
A long string of letters: DNA (4 letters, A/C/G/T) or protein (20 letters, one per amino acid).
Gene and codon
A gene is a stretch of DNA that codes for a protein; the cell reads it in 3-letter chunks called codons, one codon per amino acid.
Alignment
Sliding two sequences against each other to line up matching letters, adding gaps where one has letters the other lacks.
Match, mismatch, gap
A column where the two letters agree, a column where they disagree, and a column where one sequence has a dash (an inserted or deleted letter).
Indel (insertion / deletion)
A stretch of letters present in one sequence and missing from the other. It shows up as gaps.
Global vs local alignment
Global lines the sequences up end to end; local finds only the single best-matching stretch and ignores the rest.
Substitution matrix (BLOSUM, PAM)
A table of how good or bad every amino-acid swap is, learned from real evolution. Chemically similar swaps barely hurt; drastic ones are penalized.
Gap penalty (linear vs affine)
How much a gap costs. Linear charges the same per letter; affine charges a big fee to open a gap plus a small fee to extend it, which matches how real indels happen in a single event.
Score
The one number the aligner maximizes: rewards for matches minus penalties for mismatches and gaps. It depends entirely on the numbers you choose.
Percent identity
The fraction of aligned columns that match, with gap columns counted against you. A plain measure of similarity.
E-value
The chance that two random sequences of the same size would score this well by luck. A tiny E-value means the match is almost certainly real, not coincidence.
Homologous and conserved
Homologous means descended from a common ancestor. Conserved means kept nearly unchanged by evolution, usually because it is functionally important.
Synonymous vs missense mutation
Synonymous is a DNA change that leaves the amino acid the same (silent); missense is a DNA change that swaps the amino acid.
Purifying selection
Evolution filtering out harmful mutations, which keeps important sequences conserved over time.
