Gap model

Affine gap. A gap of length L costs open + extend × (L−1): a bigger one-time penalty to start a gap, then a small charge per extra base. Because starting a gap is expensive but continuing one is cheap, a single long indel (one mutational event) beats many scattered short gaps. Linear scoring, with one flat cost per gap column, cannot make that distinction.

The lesson

A higher score is not a better alignment

Drop the gap penalty toward zero and the optimizer discovers it can trade every mismatch for a tiny gap, reaching a higher score and “100% identity” by shredding the biology. It is technically optimal for those parameters and completely wrong.

Notice the “bad” alignment reports the higher identity. That is the whole point: percent identity, read on its own, rewarded the vandalism, which is exactly why the gap statistics next to it matter.

DNA vs protein

SARS-CoV vs SARS-CoV-2 spike: the synonymous-mutation story

Align the spike of the 2003 SARS virus and SARS-CoV-2 two ways (as coding DNA, then as the protein it encodes) and ask where they disagree.

Aligned spike codons
Nucleotide substitutions by codon position

The DNA alignment sees hundreds more differences than the protein does. Most of the codons that differ are synonymous (the DNA changed but the amino acid did not), and those substitutions pile up at the third (“wobble”) codon position, exactly where the genetic code is most degenerate. This is the fingerprint of purifying selection: changes that would alter the protein are weeded out, so the protein stays more conserved than its own gene.

The punchline: the DNA alignment counts how much the sequence changed; the protein alignment shows how much of that change could actually matter. Synonymous mutations are the gap between those two numbers. To find the mutations that matter, align the protein.

The central question

So how do you quantify alignment quality?

If the score itself moves with the parameters, what do you trust instead? The score is only the optimizer's objective, not a measure of truth. "Quality" asks a different question: did the alignment recover the real evolutionary or structural correspondence between the two sequences? Here are five checks, each grounded in biology, and most of them run right here.

1 · The raw score is not comparable across settings

Take one fixed alignment and rescore it under different parameters. Not a single letter moves, yet the number changes; doubling the match reward roughly doubles the score. So a bigger score never means a better alignment unless the scoring scheme is held fixed.

Example. The same human vs mouse HBB alignment (83.5% identical, no gaps) scores 293 with match 1 but 1,773 with match 5. Identical biology, six times the number.

2 · Scale-free descriptors help, but never identity alone

Percent identity, gap count, longest gap, and coverage do not depend on the reward units, so they travel across scoring schemes. But identity by itself is easy to game: you can manufacture a high number by stuffing an alignment with gaps so that no two mismatched residues ever share a column.

Example. The "bad" human/mouse HBB alignment reaches a perfect 100% identity, but only by opening 103 tiny gaps and stretching the alignment from 444 to 507 columns. The honest alignment is 83.5% identity with 2 gaps. Identity, gaps, and coverage together expose the fake; identity alone rewards it.

3 · Is it better than chance? (statistical significance)

A real alignment should score far above what random sequences of the same length and composition can reach. Shuffle one sequence many times, align each shuffle, and measure how many standard deviations the real score sits above that null distribution. This is the reasoning behind BLAST's E-value: the question is not "is the number big?" but "could this have happened by chance?"

4 · Does it respect the reading frame? (coding DNA)

Real insertions and deletions in a protein-coding gene almost always come in multiples of three, because a length change that is not a multiple of three shifts the reading frame and scrambles every downstream codon. An alignment of coding DNA littered with 1- and 2-base gaps is almost certainly a scoring artifact, however high it scores.

Example. In the SARS spike the two genes differ in length by 18 amino acids, which is exactly 54 nucleotides: an in-frame indel, precisely what selection on a functional protein predicts.

5 · Does it agree with biology you did not score for?

The strongest test is external agreement. Conserved motifs, catalytic residues, and cysteines that pair into disulfide bonds should line up; the alignment should match a structure-based superposition or a curated reference. None of that information is in the scoring, so agreement is genuine evidence.

Example. In the spike alignment the C-terminal ...KFDEDDSEPVLKGVKLHYT lines up perfectly in both viruses. The aligner was never told this region is conserved; it recovered a real biological anchor on its own.

Bottom line. There is no parameter-free definition of alignment quality, because alignment is inference (a hypothesis about shared ancestry), not measurement. The parameters are your evolutionary model: how often substitutions happen versus indels. A good alignment is one whose model fits the sequences and whose result survives the checks above; the score only tells you it was optimal for the model you chose. Quality is whether you chose the right model.

Stretch

Why gaps need smarter scoring

Try to find one gap penalty that both allows a single long, realistic indel and discourages lots of scattered short gaps. You cannot, and here is the proof.

My gap scoring is linear: a gap of length L costs g × L. So one gap of length 6 costs exactly the same as three scattered gaps of length 2. The scoring only sees total gap length, so it is blind to whether gaps are one event or scattered. Watch the tie:

Biology disagrees: a single 6-base indel is one mutational event, far likelier than six independent single-base indels. The fix is affine gaps: a big one-time open penalty plus a small extend penalty per extra base, so one long gap pays open once while many short gaps each pay it. Flip the tool’s gap model to Affine and re-run the “bad” presets to feel the difference.
